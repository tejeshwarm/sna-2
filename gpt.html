Twitter and the Projection of Political Personalities in India

Introduction Data & Methods Analysis Chowkidar Case Conclusion Future

1. Introduction

Overview of the study and central claim

The paper investigates how political personalities are projected on Twitter in India, with a focus on the 2019 general election year. By conducting a year-long analysis of trending political hashtags, the study reveals that online political discourse in India is heavily centred around personalities, particularly Prime Minister Narendra Modi, rather than non-personal themes. It further shows that hashtags serve as discursive tools, framing leaders in ways that allow users to project subjective interpretations, creating a mediated form of political distortion in digital spaces.

2. Research Context

Why this matters — social media & Indian politics

Twitter has emerged as a significant political communication tool in India since the 2014 general elections. Previous studies have documented:

  • How fringe politicians gain visibility through social media.
  • The role of influencers and automation in shaping narratives.
  • The prevalence of polarising content and political branding.

The authors argue that beyond disinformation or tangible political events, discursive framing — where political figures are associated with imaginative characteristics — is another strategic method to influence perceptions.

3. Research Questions

The paper focuses on three primary questions
  1. What share do political personalities occupy within digital politics in India?
  2. How are political figures projected within digital politics?
  3. How do these projections complicate our understanding of digital politics?

4. Data Collection Process

A five-step methodology used by the authors

The research follows a five-step methodology:

4.1 Gathering Hashtags

Source: Twitter’s trending list for India.
Timeframe: 1 January 2019 to 31 December 2019.
Collected: Top five hashtags per day, based on their longest trending duration.
Total hashtags: 1815, of which 55 were non-English and excluded.
Final dataset: 1760 hashtags.

4.2 Thematic Classification

Using Marchetti & Ceccobelli’s (2016) classification, hashtags were coded into categories:

  • Entertainment
  • Pointless Babble
  • News Story
  • Sports
  • Domestic Politics
  • International Politics
  • Technology
  • Geolocation
  • Religion
  • Recurring Events

Only one category was assigned per hashtag, determined by contextual meaning.

4.3 Domestic Politics Sub-Categorisation

From 1760 hashtags, 586 related to Domestic Politics. These were further coded into:

  • BJP
  • Advantaging BJP
  • Disadvantaging BJP
  • Opposition
  • Advantaging Opposition
  • Disadvantaging Opposition
  • Neutral
  • Social Justice & Empowerment
  • Crimes
  • Religious Politics
  • Elections

4.4 Collecting Tweet-Level Data

For hashtags under Domestic Politics:

  • Tweets were collected using Twitter’s Academic API.
  • Timeframe: ±12 hours around the trending period.
  • Data points: Username, engagement metrics, timestamps, tweet IDs, URLs.
  • Counted: Number of tweets per user per hashtag.

4.5 Personality-Focused Grouping

Subthemes were further grouped into personality vs. non-personality categories:

  • Narendra Modi
  • Rahul Gandhi
  • Gandhi Family
  • Congress Leadership
  • BJP Leadership
  • Hindutva Leadership
  • Regional Personalities
  • Other Personalities
  • Non-Personalities

Coding was done independently by both authors, with 95% agreement.

5. Empirical Analysis

Key patterns observed across 2019

5.1 Overall Trends

Domestic Politics dominated Twitter in 2019, accounting for 33% of hashtags and 34% of trending time. Entertainment, Sports, and Pointless Babble were consistent non-political trends. International Politics and Technology appeared sporadically.

5.2 Subtheme Observations

Hashtags advantaging BJP far outnumbered all other categories, especially before the April–May 2019 elections. Hashtags disadvantaging the opposition ranked second. Opposition-supportive hashtags were rare — only 15.6% of domestic politics hashtags.

Interpretation: The pattern shows a disproportionate presence of pro-dispensation trends, particularly in the election build-up, indicating strategic mediation and amplification.

6. Personality-Centric Trends

Who dominates the discourse and how they are framed

6.1 Modi’s Dominance

Modi was central in 61.5% of hashtags advantaging BJP. Even in hashtags disadvantaging BJP, Modi remained a key figure (35.7% share).

Example: #MainBhiChowkidar campaign — Modi adopted the term "chowkidar" (watchman) as a metaphor, inviting citizens to identify as protectors of the nation. This transformed into a mass social media movement, with BJP leaders adding “Chowkidar” to their Twitter handles and supporters updating their profile pictures.

6.2 Rahul Gandhi’s Projection

Rahul Gandhi featured prominently in both supportive and critical hashtags.

  • Supportive hashtags: #RahulForBehtarBharat, #IStandWithRahulGandhi.
  • Critical hashtags: #PappuDiwas, #RahulGandhiPagalHai — ridicule as a delegitimising tool.

6.3 Regional and Other Personalities

Opposition discourse often focused on Gandhi family members, regional leaders, and Congress leadership. Regional figures were particularly relevant in state-level politics and election periods.

7. Case Study: The Chowkidar Campaign

How a discursive frame became a movement

Origin

Response to opposition’s “Chowkidar Chor Hai” slogan linked to Rafale deal allegations.

Tactics

  • Personal rebranding (adding 'Chowkidar' to handles).
  • Pledges and profile picture frames to create visual unity.
  • Automated personalised replies from Modi’s account to supporters.

Impact

Created a shared identity between leader and supporters, fostering emotional attachment and mobilisation. It demonstrates how a discursive frame can be strategically pushed to become a mass-identifying symbol.

8. Discursive Framing

How leaders are associated with open-ended traits

Modi was often associated with abstract traits: #56InchRocks (strength), #DeshKiShaanModi (pride of the nation), #NewIndia (development vision). These traits are open-ended, allowing users to interpret meaning based on personal worldviews. Similarly, opposition figures were framed through ridicule or outsider status (#GoBackModi, #GoBackRahul).

Crucial point: These are not strictly factual statements; they are symbolic anchors that users project personal meanings onto, producing a subjective collective narrative.

9. Interpretation of Trends

What the patterns imply for digital politics

Twitter discourse in India is personality-driven. Leader-centric politics reinforces presidential-style campaigning, diminishing party identity. Hashtags serve as tools for both political mobilisation and narrative distortion.

10. Conclusion

Summary of core findings
  • Personalities dominate Indian political Twitter discourse, especially Modi.
  • Discursive framing — attaching imaginative, symbolic traits — is a deliberate strategy.
  • This method of mediated discourse is a distinct form of political distortion, different from direct disinformation.
  • Twitter is actively used to mould political perceptions, particularly in election years.

11. Future Research Directions

Where to go next
  • Investigating discursive framing in non-election years.
  • Studying the interplay between automation, coordination, and personality-based campaigns.
  • Comparing personality-driven discourse across different countries with similar political cultures.
